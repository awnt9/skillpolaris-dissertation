\subsection{Motivation}
\label{sec:motivation}

\IEEEPARstart{C}{areer} decision-making is increasingly shaped by cross-profession and cross-sector mobility into labor markets that are both complex and difficult to navigate.
This thesis is motivated by that tension in the software labor market, and by the resulting need for objective, evidence-based career intelligence rather than generic advice.

A first source of friction is informational complexity.
Occupational labels in software are numerous, and vacancies that share a similar title often request substantially different skill bundles.
Prior analyses of early-career information systems advertisements already documented heterogeneous clusters of technical and non-technical requirements within ostensibly related roles~\cite{Kennan2008}.
More recent work shows that Science, Technology, Engineering, and Mathematics (STEM) vacancies change especially quickly: technological change introduces new job skills and renders older ones obsolete, which helps explain perceived STEM shortages together with rapid skill churn over workers' careers~\cite{DemingNoray2018}.
Complementary survey evidence confirms that online job advertisements are unstructured text and that skill identification from such postings remains a non-trivial research problem, with substantial variation in skill bases, extraction methods, and granularity~\cite{Khaouja2021}.
In parallel, labor-market returns have increasingly favored combinations of technical and social skills, further widening the gap between a job title and the portfolio of competencies that employers actually screen for~\cite{Deming2017}.
Taken together, these findings imply that entrants and career changers face an opaque signal: the market is not a single stable ``programmer'' profile, but a moving distribution of role-specific demands.

A second source of friction is difficulty at the point of entry for early-career professionals.
Comparative survey evidence indicates that faculty in information technology (IT)-related programmes and IT managers in industry do not fully agree on which knowledge and skills matter most for entry-level workers, which complicates preparation for the first job~\cite{Aasheim2009}.
Against heterogeneous vacancy requirements and rapid STEM skill change~\cite{Kennan2008,DemingNoray2018}, newcomers therefore face a high informational and strategic cost when deciding where to invest scarce learning time.
Task-based models of technical change emphasize that technology does not eliminate occupations wholesale; it reallocates tasks between capital and labor and reshapes the skill content of work~\cite{AcemogluAutor2010,AcemogluRestrepo2019}.
Recent administrative evidence is consistent with that reallocation biting hardest at the entry point: after the diffusion of generative artificial intelligence, early-career workers (ages 22--25) in the most AI-exposed occupations---including software development---experienced a roughly 16\% relative employment decline compared with less exposed occupations, even after controlling for firm-level shocks, while employment for more experienced workers in the same fields remained more stable~\cite{BrynjolfssonChandarChen2025}.
Complementary vacancy data for software roles likewise show a post-ChatGPT decline of about 14--15\% in junior postings relative to senior ones~\cite{ModestinoWestbyCheng2026}.
Together, these patterns suggest that the current conjuncture of programming assistants does not merely change tools; it tightens the labor-market doorway that early-career entrants must pass through.

Despite that difficulty, people continue to enter programming pathways.
This apparent paradox can be interpreted through the economic logic of rebound effects---classically associated with Jevons' observation that efficiency improvements may increase rather than reduce overall resource use~\cite{vandenBergh2010}---applied here by analogy to software production.
If tools that raise the productivity of producing software make software cheaper relative to alternatives, demand for software-intensive products and services expands, and new complementary tasks emerge.
In the labor framework of Acemoglu and Restrepo, such expansion corresponds to a reinstatement channel that can sustain or grow demand for human work even as specific tasks are automated~\cite{AcemogluRestrepo2019}.
On a medium-term horizon, therefore, software-related competence remains a rational destination for many knowledge-work transitions, even while long-horizon automation may eventually reach further into cognitive tasks if reinstatement weakens.

These two motives are complementary rather than contradictory.
Structural demand for software-related skills continues to pull workers toward the field, while skill heterogeneity, rapid obsolescence, and shifting task content make the market costly to interpret---especially for early-career entrants and those migrating from other sectors.

\subsection{Problem Statement}
\label{sec:problem-statement}

The motivational frictions above create a concrete information need: early-career and transitioning workers must infer what the software labor market currently demands, and how their own skill profiles relate to that demand, without relying on generic advice.
Existing research already shows that online vacancies are a rich but unstructured source for skill identification and market monitoring~\cite{Khaouja2021}.
In parallel, deployed recruitment systems typically optimize a different objective---matching candidates to individual job postings through recommender-style retrieval and ranking~\cite{Zhao2021,Alsaif2022}.
What remains insufficiently addressed for career \emph{strategy} is a user-facing layer that turns vacancy data into \emph{inspectable, reproducible aggregates} conditioned on the user's profile---from which individuals can draw their own conclusions---rather than a ranked list of openings or a narrative recommendation.

Accordingly, the engineering problem addressed in this thesis is to design and implement a system that (i)~acquires software job offers and transforms each into structured metadata together with an ideal-candidate representation, and (ii)~given a user profile, selects a proximity cohort in that representation space and exposes deterministic statistical metrics over the cohort's metadata to support decisions such as where aptitude is highest and where scarce learning time yields the best expected return.

Two scope decisions delimit this problem.
First, the intended outputs are objective aggregations, not a conversational retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) assistant and not a vacancy recommender as the primary interface: embeddings may be used to define a relevant cohort, but the answers returned to the user are corpus-derived statistics (for example, frequent standardized role titles in the cohort, stack-fit rates, or typical experience requirements), not generative advice and not a shortlist of jobs to apply to.
A production-grade conversational layer on top of the same dataset is treated as future work of separate scope; the value of the present contribution is placed in the enriched dataset and in the metrics derived from it.
Second, the domain is restricted to the programmer~/~software labor market.
Offers in this segment tend to share a relatively homogeneous surface form (role title, technology stack, and compensation-related signals), which makes systematic extraction, structuring, and aggregation tractable; whether the same pipeline transfers unchanged to less homogeneous occupations is left open and is not claimed here.
This domain choice is also consistent with the motivational focus on software entry and mid-career migration developed in Section~\ref{sec:motivation}.

The proposed solution shape is therefore an offline acquisition and transformation pipeline, plus an application that performs profile-conditioned aggregation over a retrieved cohort and presents the resulting metrics to the user.
Implementation choices, non-functional requirements, and design alternatives are developed in later chapters; the introduction only fixes the problem, its boundaries, and this high-level solution outline.

\subsection{Research Objectives}
\label{sec:research-objectives}

The general objective of this thesis is to design and implement a labor-market intelligence system for the software domain that acquires vacancies, transforms them into metadata and ideal-candidate representations, and exposes deterministic, reproducible statistical metrics over a profile-conditioned cohort so that users can form their own career conclusions---in particular, assessing occupational fit and prioritizing learning investments---without relying on a conversational recommender or generative advice layer.

At the product level, success is understood proximally as enabling sound career decisions from inspectable market information; long-horizon professional outcomes are acknowledged as the ultimate aspiration of such a system but are not treated as directly measurable targets within this thesis.
The evaluable objectives below therefore focus on building the pipeline and on verifying that the derived metrics are feasible, faithful to the underlying data, and inspectable.

\begin{enumerate}
  \item Design and implement an acquisition pipeline that collects software-related job offers from selected sources into a consolidated store, under practical constraints of cost, legality, and operational reliability (including idempotent ingestion and a standardized offer representation).
  \item Design and implement a transformation stage that, for each offer, produces recruiter-oriented metadata suitable for aggregation and an ideal-candidate representation suitable for embedding-based proximity search, including reliable treatment of standardized role~/~job-title information.
  \item Define and expose profile-conditioned statistical metrics---computed by retrieving a neighborhood of ideal candidates for a user profile and aggregating that cohort's metadata---through an application interface that presents inspectable aggregates rather than ranked vacancy recommendations or LLM-generated narratives.
  \item Evaluate the pipeline and metrics with respect to transform quality, correctness and stability of the aggregations, and feasibility within the target market, using instruments appropriate to those claims (for example, a curated reference sample and deterministic recomputation checks), within the scope limits fixed in Section~\ref{sec:problem-statement}.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Contributions}
\label{sec:contributions}

The main contributions of this thesis are summarized as follows:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item An acquisition and transformation pipeline for software job vacancies that produces, for each offer, recruiter-oriented metadata and an ideal-candidate representation suitable for indexing and later aggregation.
  \item A guidance approach based on \emph{profile-conditioned aggregation}: given a user profile, a proximity cohort of ideal candidates is retrieved and statistical metrics are computed over that cohort's metadata, yielding an information layer for career strategy rather than vacancy recommendations or conversational RAG answers.
  \item An application surface that presents those metrics in inspectable form (for example, frequent standardized roles in the cohort, stack-fit indicators, and related distributional summaries), so that users can draw their own conclusions from evidence grounded in the corpus.
  \item An evaluation design aligned with the stated claims---centered on transform quality, aggregation correctness and stability, and feasibility in the target market---while treating long-horizon career outcomes as proximal product vision rather than as a directly measured experimental endpoint.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Working Hypothesis}
\label{sec:working-hypothesis}

The working hypothesis of this thesis is that, for career-strategy questions in the software labor market, \emph{profile-conditioned statistical aggregates}---obtained by retrieving a proximity cohort of ideal-candidate representations and summarizing that cohort's structured metadata---provide inspectable and reproducible market evidence that is better aligned with informed individual decision-making than either (i)~vacancy-level recommendation rankings or (ii)~conversational retrieval-augmented generation over the same corpus.

In operational terms, the hypothesis predicts that a pipeline which couples offline enrichment (metadata and ideal candidates) with deterministic cohort aggregations can be implemented with measurable transform quality and aggregation fidelity, and that the resulting indicators remain stable enough under reasonable cohort-size choices to support interpretation by the user.
Confirmation is sought through the evaluation instruments outlined in the objectives and contributions (reference samples, recomputation checks, and related feasibility analyses), not through longitudinal observation of employment outcomes.
